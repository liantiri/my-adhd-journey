Section 01

The symptoms,
mapped.

Each pattern below comes directly from lived experience and is matched against DSM-5 diagnostic criteria for ADHD. Tap any card to expand.

Inattentive cluster
Strong match Fails to sustain attention — leaves tasks unfinished

New Word documents written constantly — with genuine enthusiasm — then never saved or finished. This isn't laziness; it's the dopamine of the idea evaporating the moment execution begins.

Pattern repeats across school (never had complete books), IT Engineering (quit), foundation degree (repeated semesters, never attended lectures), and diploma (studied hours before 6 months of content).

"I write up new Word documents with new ideas always but never finish or save the document. It stays unsaved always on my laptop."

Strong match Mind hijacked by extraneous thoughts mid-task

The mind branches into 10 different thoughts — related and unrelated — in under 10 seconds mid-conversation, before snapping back. This is involuntary, not a choice.

Carries a plate to get water, realizes mid-journey the reason for the trip. The working memory simply didn't hold the original intention long enough.

"My mind goes to 10 different related and unrelated thoughts in under 10 seconds and I snap back into reality."

Strong match Chronic forgetfulness across all areas of life

Constantly losing phones, wallets, keys, and powerbanks — spending hours searching. Routine things forgotten repeatedly despite knowing they matter.

Most telling: attended a repeat university exam entirely unprepared — not because of avoidance, but because the exam had genuinely been forgotten while working.

"I went to the exam without studying cus I was working at that time and completely didn't remember I had a subject to finish off."

Strong match Procrastination driven by inability to initiate

Last-minute studying is not a bad habit — it is the ADHD nervous system finally activating under genuine urgency. The brain requires interest, urgency, or pressure to initiate effort.

Studied 6–8 months of diploma content in hours before the exam and passed. The knowledge was always there. The activation wasn't. Tried and tested many solutions to procrastination — none worked sustainably.

"I need stress, pressure, urgency, fear to get things done."

Strong match Poor time perception — "time blindness"

Scheduling meetings back to back with no buffer — not from arrogance but from a genuine inability to feel how long things take. The future doesn't register as real until it's nearly now.

Family consistently remarks on this. It is one of the most consistent complaints across years and relationships.

"I have no sense of time. Family complains."

Hyperactive / impulsive cluster
Strong match Physical restlessness — can't stay still

Leg tapping and fidgeting are constant. Difficulty sitting in one place cited as one of the reasons for leaving IT and coding — the environment demanded stillness the body refused.

Was in sports 24/7 during school — not just interest, but a nervous system that needed movement to function.

"I step/tap my leg many times, like fidgeting. I always want to move. One of the reasons why I stopped coding."

Strong match Impulsivity and emotional intensity

Short temper confirmed by family and recognized personally — emotions arrive fast and at full volume before the prefrontal cortex can moderate them. This is emotional dysregulation, not a character flaw.

Risk-seeking and novelty-seeking behavior: drawn to stimulating, high-stakes experiences. Boredom isn't just unpleasant — it's intolerable.

"I love taking risks or things that stimulate me that is very risky to do."

Strong match Tangential speech — random topic shifts

Friends have specifically pointed out the pattern of introducing completely unrelated topics mid-conversation — especially during high-energy states. The brain makes an internal connection and the thought must come out immediately or it disappears.

"Friends have pointed out I bring random topics totally unrelated while talking about a subject — when I'm high energy."

Present Hyperfocus and novelty-seeking

Gets intensely interested in new topics, businesses, and ideas — then moves on when the novelty fades. Watches content at 2x speed because normal pace can't sustain attention. Daydreams extensively.

This is the flip side of ADHD: when something genuinely engages the brain, the focus locks in completely. It just can't be summoned on demand.

"I look for new things, new topics, new businesses. I search for novelty and get interested in it."

Sensory and environmental
Notable Sensory overload in crowded environments

In environments with many people, all senses heighten simultaneously — sight, hearing, smell. Everything gets noticed. The brain cannot filter irrelevant stimuli and exhausts itself trying to process all of it at once.

Notably, the brother experiences the same thing — sensory sensitivity has a strong genetic component and frequently co-occurs with ADHD.

"All my senses are heightened and active, I see and notice everything, hear everything around, smell everything. I get very tired in situations like that."

Notable Mental fatigue from internal noise

Hundreds of simultaneous thoughts throughout the day create profound mental tiredness — not from external demands but from the sheer volume of internal activity. The brain never fully rests.

This was described directly to a partner as "a warzone" — one of the most accurate self-descriptions of the ADHD internal experience documented in the literature.

"During the day I get very taxed/tired by the 100s of thoughts in my head."

Section 03

A life in patterns.

ADHD is not a phase. Every stage of life tells the same story — the tools changed, the pattern didn't.

Grades 1–9
Top 3 in class — on pure instinct
Never had complete books. Studied the day before exams alone. Still outperformed peers. Intelligence compensated for systems that weren't working. Sport was constant — the body demanded movement.
Grades 9–12
The cram strategy hits its limit
Volume of content finally exceeded what hours of cramming could cover. Grades declined. The same approach that worked brilliantly for years stopped scaling. Didn't complete A-levels.
University Foundation
Passed everything — except what required consistency
Passed subjects with self-made theories, studied hours before. Failed maths — the one subject where formulas had to be memorized over time. Repeated twice. Forgot an exam entirely while working. Never attended lectures in repeat semesters. Still passed above others in the final attempt.
Relationships
The warzone no one could see inside
Communication gaps throughout. Told partners: "my mind is like a warzone, I don't think you'd understand." Disagreements rooted not in lack of care but in inability to translate the internal chaos into words in real-time. First breakup triggered months of depression — evidence of intense emotional sensitivity.
Adult life
The pattern is everywhere now
Unfinished documents. Lost items. Meetings scheduled badly. Exhaustion from crowds. Racing thoughts at night. Novelty-chasing. Boredom intolerance. The same ADHD nervous system — in a world that never built systems for it.

Section 05

What comes next.

Self-recognition is the first step. A clinical diagnosis is the one that opens doors.

Step 1 See a psychiatrist for formal assessment

A psychiatrist — privately or through a government hospital — can conduct a formal ADHD assessment. In Sri Lanka, both routes are available. You don't need a referral to start this conversation.

Bring this document. Say: "I've been recognizing these patterns throughout my life and I'd like a formal assessment for ADHD." That is all it takes.

Step 2 Complete the ASRS v1.1 screener

The Adult ADHD Self-Report Scale (ASRS v1.1) is an 18-question WHO-endorsed screener. It's the tool many psychiatrists use themselves as a first step. A high score is a strong signal to take to an appointment.

Available free online: search "ASRS v1.1 screener Harvard."

Step 3 Explore what management looks like for you

ADHD is not cured, but it is very treatable. Options include: stimulant medication (methylphenidate, amphetamine salts), non-stimulant medication, CBT adapted for ADHD, and lifestyle strategies like structured exercise.

Many people describe their first effective ADHD medication as the first time they understood what "quiet" felt like inside their own head.
